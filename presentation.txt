1. Purpose of the Final Presentation
The final presentation represents the culmination of your work in CIS354. It is not only a demonstration of your application, but also an evaluation of your ability to communicate your development process, decisions, and outcomes.

As outlined in the course expectations, students must deliver a completed and demonstrable project and present it to their peers :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}.

Your presentation should clearly answer:

What did you build?
How does it work?
What challenges did you face?
Did you meet your goals?
What would you improve?
2. Presentation Flow
Introduction

Application Overview

Live Demonstration

Challenges

Budget Review

Results

Success Factors

Reflection and Improvements

Your presentation should follow a logical sequence. Avoid jumping between topics. The audience should be able to follow your journey from concept to outcome.

3. Step 1 - Application Overview
Begin by explaining your application at a high level.

What problem does your application solve?
Who is the intended user?
What platforms are included?
What are the key features?
Keep this concise. The goal is to prepare the audience for your demonstration.

4. Step 2 - Live Demonstration
Demonstrate your application in action. This is the most important part of your presentation.

Show core functionality
Walk through a realistic user scenario
Demonstrate data interaction where applicable
Highlight multiple platforms if applicable
Focus on what works. If something fails, explain it clearly rather than avoiding it.
5. Step 3 - Challenges
Discuss the major challenges encountered during development.

Technical challenges
Design challenges
Team or communication challenges
Unexpected blockers
Explain how you addressed these challenges. This demonstrates problem-solving ability.

6. Step 4 - Budget Review
Present your development budget using Actual Hours vs Estimated Hours.

Which requirements consumed the most time?
Where did estimates differ from reality?
Were there overruns or efficiencies?
This connects your presentation to project management outcomes and demonstrates your ability to track progress.

7. Step 5 - Results
Clearly state the outcome of your project.

Was the project completed as planned?
Which features are fully implemented?
Which features are incomplete or missing?
Be honest. Partial completion is acceptable if clearly explained.

8. Step 6 - Success Factors
Success factors explain why your project succeeded or progressed as expected.

Strong planning or architecture
Effective teamwork
Clear requirements
Efficient tools or technologies
If your project did not meet expectations, redefine success in terms of learning and progress.

9. Step 7 - Reflection and Improvements
If your project was not completed as expected, explain what you would do differently next time.

What would you change in your planning?
What technical decisions would you revisit?
How would you manage time differently?
This is not a failure discussion. It is a professional reflection on improvement.
10. Presentation Expectations
Clear and structured delivery
Professional communication
Demonstration readiness
Balanced participation among team members
Time management
Your ability to communicate your work is just as important as the work itself.
